Fri · Jun 26🌲 Big Nature Day — pick one
🏔️ Option A · Mount RainierBig day
Leave ~7:30 AM for Paradise (~2.5 hr each way). The bucket-list pick — but only if the forecast is clear, or the mountain just hides in the clouds. Late June means lingering snow up top and wildflowers not out yet (those peak late July–Aug). No timed-entry reservation needed in 2026; arrive before 7 AM for parking or take a guided tour.
📍 Paradise, Mount Rainier National Park, WA 98397Map ↗
🍄 Foraging: Picking is banned inside the national park. Forage only in the surrounding Gifford Pinchot National Forest (Ashford / Packwood side) with a free personal-use permit from a ranger station. Late-June morels linger at lower elevations near Ashford & Greenwater — hunt burn areas & stream corridors. It's the tail end of the season, so treat it as a long-shot bonus, not the plan.
💧 Option B · Snoqualmie FallsHalf day
Only ~35 min from downtown — a dramatic 270-ft waterfall with short, kid-friendly viewpoints and the Salish Lodge perched above. Light enough to pair with a city afternoon, and the reliable choice if the weather's iffy.
📍 Snoqualmie Falls, 6501 Railroad Ave SE, Snoqualmie, WA 98065Map ↗
🍄 Foraging: The falls themselves are private (PSE / Salish Lodge) — no picking there. But the surrounding Mt. Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest along I-90 (North Bend → Snoqualmie Pass) allows personal-use foraging with a free permit from the ranger district (425-783-6000). Higher-elevation morels can still pop in late June. No harvesting in wilderness or closed areas.
⛴️ Option C · Bainbridge IslandLow-key
The relaxed finale: a 35-min walk-on ferry from downtown (the skyline & Sound views are the attraction), then Winslow's walkable town and a waterfront seafood dinner. No car, cheap, easy with the kids — and you're back whenever. The best call the night before you fly out.
📍 Seattle Ferry Terminal (Colman Dock), 801 Alaskan Way, Seattle, WA 98104Map ↗
🍄 Foraging: Bainbridge is mostly managed parks & preserves (Grand Forest, Bloedel Reserve) where collecting is restricted — it's not a real forage spot, and late June is off-season here. Come for the ferry & the seafood, not the mushrooms.
🍄 Forager's golden rules: Always pull the free national-forest permit first (national parks = never pick). And never eat a wild mushroom without a 100% positive ID — toxic false morels (Gyromitra) closely mimic true morels and have made people seriously ill. If you're not certain, photograph it and leave it. The safe way to learn is a guided foray or the Puget Sound Mycological Society.

    🍴Seattle Must-Try

    The local specialties — what people actually eat here — with a top spot for each. ★ = Google rating · 📺 = featured in the food videos.

    🍱 Seattle-style Teriyaki

    A Seattle original — sweet-savory grilled chicken or beef over rice, in teriyaki shops all over town. Try: Okinawa Teriyaki 📺 (downtown), Grillbird ★4.7 (West Seattle), or Rainier Teriyaki ★4.6.

    ☕ Coffee

    The city runs on it — skip the chains for a local roaster. Try: gloom coffee ★5.0 & The Good Coffee Co ★4.9 (both downtown), Espresso Vivace, or a photo at the original Starbucks.

    🐟 Wild Salmon

    Pacific salmon — grilled, cedar-planked, or as lox. Try: Market Grill (Pike Place salmon sandwich), Local Tide ★4.8, or Ivar's on the water.

    🦀 Dungeness Crab

    Sweet, meaty local crab — a Pacific NW must. Try: Lowell's 📺 at Pike Place — crab with a bay view.

    🦪 Pacific Oysters

    Cold, briny, just-shucked. Try: The Walrus and the Carpenter ★4.6 (Ballard) or Taylor Shellfish.

    🥪 Corned Beef Deli

    Old-school stacked sandwiches. Try: Market House Meats 📺 ★4.8 — a Seattle institution since 1933.

    🍗 Fried Chicken

    Crispy, juicy, NW-style. Try: Cookie's Country Chicken 📺 ★4.7 (Pioneer Sq) or Fat's Chicken & Waffles ★4.6.

    🍦 Ice Cream

    Local, seasonal flavors. Try: Molly Moon's 📺, Salt & Straw ★4.7, or Hellenika Greek creamery at Pike Place.

    🍕 Pizza

    Seattle's pizza game is serious. Try: A.K. Pizza 📺 (the video's best slice) or Serious Pie downtown.

    🍒 Pike Place in June

    Peak season: grab Rainier cherries & berries, fresh-shucked oysters, and a warm piroshky as you wander the Market.

      🧾 Basic Economy — know before you fly

      Seats are assigned at check-in (you may not sit together unless you pay), you board in the last group, and you get one free personal item that fits under the seat — a carry-on or checked bag costs extra. Check in right at the 24-hour mark for the best shot at seats together, and gate-check strollers/car seats free.

      🔄Changing Planes at DFW

      Where you catch "the 2nd plane." Good news: all your American flights use DFW Terminals A, B & C, which are linked inside security by the free Skylink train — you do not exit or re-do TSA between flights.

      ⏱️ Outbound: the 40-min sprint Tight

      The moment you land (DFW 7:40 AM):
      1. Step off and look at the nearest screen / "Gate Information" monitor for AA 767 to Seattle — note its gate (e.g., B12, C20).
      2. Same terminal letter? Just walk to the gate.
      3. Different terminal? Follow Skylink signs up to the platform — trains come every ~2 min and reach any terminal in ~5–9 min. Do not leave the secure area or take the slower TownLink shuttle.
      4. Head straight to the gate — with a 40-min connection, boarding may already be underway.

      💡 Make the tight connection easier

      · Open the AA app before you land — it shows your connecting gate and a terminal map.
      · Use the restroom on the plane, not in the terminal.
      · At check-in, try to grab seats near the front of AA 2789 so you're off first.
      · If you're nervous about 40 min with kids, ask an AA agent about an earlier DFW→SEA flight as backup.
      · Your bags are checked through to SEA — you don't collect them at DFW.

      🍽️ Return: 2 hours to spare

      You land DFW 4:32 PM and don't leave until 6:30 PM (gate AA 2263). Plenty of time — find your gate first, then grab dinner. DFW Terminals A–C have lots of Texas BBQ, Tex-Mex, and grab-and-go.

      ✈️SEA Airport → Hotel

      You land at SEA ~10:49 AM. SEA is ~14 mi south of downtown — here's how to get to 400 Spring St, cheapest to easiest.

      🧭 When you land at SEA — step by step

      1. Follow Baggage Claim signs down to the lower level and grab your bags.
      2. Then follow "Link Light Rail" signs — it's a ~10-min indoor walk across the parking-garage walkway (4th floor) to SeaTac/Airport Station.
      3. Buy a ticket from the machine or tap a contactless card; take the 1 Line toward Lynnwood.
      4. Ride ~37 min to Symphony Station, then walk ~6 min uphill to the hotel. (Prefer door-to-door with bags & tired kids? Grab an Uber/Lyft from Garage level 3 instead — see below.)

      🚆 Link light rail Cheapest

      1 Line from SeaTac/Airport Station → Symphony (~37 min), then a 6-min uphill walk. ~$3/person (≈$12 for four). Trains every ~8 min. Buy ORCA or tap a card.

      🚗 Uber / Lyft Easiest

      Door-to-door ~25–40 min, ~$50–70 for the group with bags. Pickup on the ramp at Garage level 3.

      🚕 Taxi

      Curbside, 3rd floor of the garage. ~30–40 min, ~$55–65 + tip.

      🅿️ Rental car Note

      ~25 min via I-5 N, but the hotel charges $45/night for covered parking. Everything on your list is walkable — you likely don't need a car unless you do the Rainier/Olympic day trips.
